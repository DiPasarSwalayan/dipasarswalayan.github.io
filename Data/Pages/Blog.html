Local British man takes dealership hostage.


In a bizarre protest turned hostage situation, a British man known only as "Nigel T." barricaded himself inside a Genord Motors dealership early Tuesday morning, reportedly shouting "I WANT REAL ENGINES AND I WANT THEM NOW!" over the loudspeakers. Armed with a thermos of Earl Grey and a deep-rooted hatred of electric cars, Nigel claimed the Model F was "an affront to God, petrol, and proper noise."

After a tense four-hour standoff and multiple attempts to bribe him with complimentary air fresheners, police negotiators finally diffused the situation by letting Nigel test drive the Model F. Witnesses say he returned 7 minutes later with a tear in his eye, whispering "that acceleration..." before surrendering peacefully. He left the scene in a Model F, vowing to "convert the lads at the pub." Genord Motors called the event "a spontaneous customer onboarding."

European Union bans rat testing.


After months of pressure from animal rights groups and increasing media scrutiny, the European Union has issued a formal ban on all "genetically manipulative electric rat testing," citing Genord Motors as a "disturbingly creative example" of modern biotech abuse. The ruling cites "compromised ethics, bizarre breeding programs, and multiple explosions" as grounds for immediate suspension.

Genord's R&D division released a public statement saying, "The rats knew what they signed up for," though it remains unclear if rats can legally consent. As a workaround, Genord claims they are "looking into mushrooms" as a new organic power source. A leaked internal memo read simply: "Fungi can't file lawsuits." The public remains divided, with some calling the ban "a win for science ethics," and others lamenting the loss of the only lab in Europe where electricity and squeaks were scientifically fused.

New Genord model F release.


Genord Motors has officially launched the long-awaited Model F: a fully electric vehicle powered by the company's proprietary "bio-electro synergy units" - the technical term for the battery technology harvested from over 16 generations of genetically bred super rats. The vehicle boasts zero emissions, no exhaust port, and what Genord refers to as "negligible squeaking."

The Model F also omits several traditional safety features, including seatbelts, citing "aerodynamic purity." Early adopters praise the car's monthly charging cycle, minimalist design, and what one tester called a "thrill that reminds you you're alive... because you might not be for long." Pricing starts at a modest amount Genord claims "is worth it for the guilt-free horsepower alone." When asked about the lack of crash test ratings, a spokesperson simply responded: "If you crash, you did it wrong."

Cofounder found dead at the back of a Wendys.


Genord Motors cofounder Gregory Frasnik was found deceased behind a Wendy's in what authorities are calling a "tragic and mysterious end" to one of the company's most controversial visionaries. Witnesses say Frasnik had been muttering to himself about "the electric soul of the rat" in the days prior to his death. A half-eaten Frosty and a napkin scrawled with "We shouldn't have pushed them past Gen-12" were recovered at the scene.

While police investigate, Genord issued a brief and emotionally charged statement: "Gregory was a bold thinker, a pioneer in unconventional propulsion methods, and a man unafraid to weaponize biology. He will be missed - but not replaced, since no one else wanted his job anyway." Rumors of a Gen-17 rat sighting near the scene remain unconfirmed.

Whistleblower leaks internal rat efficiency charts.


A former Genord lab technician has leaked confidential documents revealing extensive charts tracking the "electric conversion rate" of over 9,000 lab rats across 21 generations. Some charts include graphs labeled "Squeak to Watt Ratio" and "Post-Implant Stability (Explosiveness Factor)." The whistleblower, hiding under the alias "Sparky," claims they left the company after being asked to "test how loud a rat could scream under voltages used in urban charging stations."

Genord dismissed the leak as "outdated data" and assured customers that "no current rats are screaming... loudly." Animal rights groups have called for an immediate investigation, while investors praised the company's "data-driven approach to bioelectrical R&D."

Genord's seatbeltless safety trials raise eyebrows.


Genord's controversial decision to remove seatbelts from the Model F in favor of "free movement" and "emotional thrill" has led to a wave of experimental crash tests. The results? Mixed. In one simulation, a dummy was ejected directly into a billboard, which then short-circuited due to the dummy's residual charge. Genord called the results "promising."

The company is now marketing the feature under the tagline: *"If you're flying, you're winning."* A company representative explained that "humans evolved without seatbelts, and we want to return to that naturalistic approach to mobility." Road safety organizations have issued strong warnings, and at least one insurance firm has added a new surcharge called "Genord Risk Premium (tm)."

Investor Q&A devolves into philosophical debate on rat consciousness.


What started as a routine quarterly investor Q&A quickly spiraled into chaos when one analyst asked, "Do the rats know they're powering cars?" Genord CEO Linda Varn responded with a pause, followed by: "Define 'know'." A 45-minute argument ensued, including multiple references to Descartes, Black Mirror, and a questionable PowerPoint slide titled "Ratception."

Despite the derailment, Genord's stock rose 3% following the meeting, with many investors praising the company's "bold refusal to engage with conventional morality." The event has since been uploaded to YouTube under the title *"Genord CEO Destroys Ethics Professor in Debate."*
